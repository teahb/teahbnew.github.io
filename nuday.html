NU Day

Studying how Northwestern students build community, Designing NU Day.


Interface Design


Winter 2016


NU Day is a mobile application that allows students to submit and support “challenges” that encourage building a positive, supportive community: both by challenging students to do nice things for others, as well as encouraging each other to be kind to themselves.



The application allows you to keep track of challenges you’d like to try by saving them to a personal list. Saving a challenge rewards the contributor by indicating how many students are interested in their idea, and transforms the intangible concept of “building community” into a tangible list.



Challenges are crowdsourced, categorized, and offered to everyone, every day. As students see others complete a challenge they noticed on NU Day, they are prompted to join in and complete the challenge themselves.

I. CHALLENGE

The Northwestern student experience is complex and diverse. Students identify with friend groups, organizations, academic departments - but oftentimes, they don’t feel recognized or supported by the larger Northwestern community. Our design project employed spontaneity, online communication trends, and acts of kindness to create a more positive sense of community for students on campus.


HOW MIGHT WE...engage Northwestern students in contributing to a positive community?


II. RESEARCH

A mind map helped up to outline the different ways that Northwestern identify themselves, and others. Through interviews and observations, we noticed how these identity groups translated into a feeling of anonymity around campus. We quickly became intrigued with the idea of randomness, especially because students tend to shy away from long-term or future commitments in the name of being “too busy.”

III. INSIGHTS

1. Students use phones as a time filler or to avoid awkward interactions.


2. Students are constantly thinking about stressors, and to-do list items - because of this, they hardly ever engage in spontaneous acts.


3. Students are hesitant to commit to attending events that are more than a week in the future.


4. Students often prioritize other forms of wellness (exercising, eating well, or maintaining a study schedule) over mental health because they are less stigmatized and more tangible.


5. Small acts of kindness can make a person’s day.


6. Students want to do nice things for others, but sometimes don’t know how, and become crippled by anxiety over new interactions.


7. It’s easier to attempt small daily tasks than long-term behavioral changes, such as New Year’s resolutions.


IV. IDEATION

Ideation focused on mobile applications that did not act as additional social platforms, but rather encouraged healthy social life offline. We were attracted to the concept of a calendar-style challenge of the day that prompted random acts of kindness. We capitalized on ideas that celebrated free bursts of time during the day: when waiting for food, arriving to class early, or traveling from A to B.


V. PROTOTYPING

To prototype, we mocked up a wireframe of NU Day, and conducted user testing. We also used the popular app Yik Yak to gauge how Northwestern students would react to the concept of a “Northwestern challenge.” We flooded the app with “NU Challenge” posts to see if positivity would be well-received, particularly when mixed among the more satirical posts on Yik Yak that usually trended to be more popular. To our surprise, by the end of the day, other anonymous Yik Yak students had contributed their own challenges, and one of our posts had risen to the most popular of the day, with over 180 upvotes. This litmus test confirmed our direction.
